    DNA metabarcoding

    DNA sequencing‐based tools are becoming one of the most popular approaches with which to detect and measure biodiversity in a wide range of natural systems1. For example, studies spanning questions from dietary assessments2 to microbiome analyses3 and ecosystem‐level biodiversity studies4 have adopted over the last decade molecular approaches based on metabarcoding. This methodological shift has changed, or at least complemented, the way we measure biodiversity5. For example, operational units for measuring diversity (i.e. types) are no longer necessarily defined through taxonomic means, and it is easier than ever to account for the degree of (dis)similarity between the types detected in diversity measurement.

    Using metabarcoding, the amplification and subsequent sequencing of short (usually between 50 and 500 nucle‐ otides) targeted molecular markers yields multiple presumably ho‐ mologous sequences that are directly comparable to each other (but see section on zero‐inflated, insufficient and biased data).

    Dietary diversity

    Dietary diversity is another relevant example, both because diet is one of the main elements defining the ecological niche of species (Sexton, Montiel, Shay, Stephens, & Slatyer, 2017), and because ecosystem functioning largely depends on trophic interactions (Thébault & Loreau, 2003).
