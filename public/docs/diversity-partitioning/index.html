    Diversity partitioning based on Hill numbers

    In ecology, the idea of diversity has been traditionally broken down into three components: alpha (α), beta (β) and gamma (γ) diversities1. In general terms, α‐diversity refers to the average diversity of subsystems or samples (although see discussion about the different α‐diversities in Chao, Chiu, & Hsieh, 20122), β‐diversity measures the differences between subsystems (although see discussion about the different β‐diversities in Tuomisto, 20103), while γ‐diversity includes the entire diversity of the system (Figure 5). Despite the existence of different approaches for diversity partitioning, within the framework of Hill number diversity partitioning responds to the multiplicative definition defined below4:

    \( ^qD_γ = ^qD_α * ^qD_ß \)

    That is, beta diversity is obtained by dividing gamma diversity by alpha diversity. This formulation has three properties that ecologists intuitively expected from a diversity measurement2:

    1. Alpha and beta components are unrelated; thus, a high value of alpha does not force the beta component to be high and vice versa.
    2. Gamma is completely determined by alpha and beta.
    3. Alpha is never greater than gamma.

    One advantage of the Hill numbers-based partitioning of diversity proposed by Jost is that alpha, beta, and gamma diversities of order 0 do not count species abundances and are the same as the incidence-based alpha, beta, and gamma diversities. Thus, incidence-based measures are simply the abundance-based measures of order zero (q=0). In consequence, it is not necessary to define additional formulas and terminology for incidence- or richness-based alpha, beta, and related measures, which simplifies diversity theory and contributes to minimising concepts and terminology2.

    Computing diversity partitioning in R

    Hill number-based diversity partitioning can be easily conducted in R using the package hilldiv:

    library(hilldiv)
div_part()

    Alpha diversity

    Alpha diversity is a measure of the mean (yet not necessarily the arithmetic mean) diversity of the study units within a system. It is necessary to note that Hill numbers-based alpha diversity is not obtained by directly averaging the Hill numbers of the subsystems, but computing the Hill numbers from the averaged basic sums of the study units2. This definition of the alpha diversity was proposed in 2006 by Lou Jost4, and later demonstrated to be the most meaningful definition by Chao et al2.
