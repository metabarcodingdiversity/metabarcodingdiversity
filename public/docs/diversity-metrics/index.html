    Weighing the importance of types

    Diversity measurements require assignment of an importance value to each of the detected types. In traditional community ecology, this has been done using metrics such as individual counts, biomass or spatial units, depending on the type of system, research question and fieldwork strategy. Molecular analyses provide a different type of data that could provide such information, namely the amount of DNA sequences assigned to each type.

    The simplest measure of diversity is type richness1. As this only considers whether a type is present or absent in the system, abundant and rare types are given the same weight. However, the multiple OTUs present in a system are seldom distributed evenly; thus, richness is rarely the best approach with which to reflect the diversity of a system. Consider for instance, a simple system characterised with 1,000 sequence reads, in which 990 belong to Type1 and 10 to Type2. This would yield a richness value of 2, even though the system is overwhelmingly dominated by Type1. Thus, metrics that also account for the evenness of the system, are considered more representative of the diversity of a system.

    When computing the diversity of biological systems considering both the number of types as well as their relative weights, there are two main possibilities: use traditional diversity indices such as the Shannon or Simpson indices, or ideally, use Hill numbers.

    Correlations across types

    The traditional and still most broadly used diversity indices, as well as their corresponding Hill numbers, assume that all types are equally (dis)similar; that is, types are treated as discrete elements with no correlation among them. Such measures have been coined as neutral or species‐neutral measures of diversity (Chao et al., 2010; Marcon & Hérault, 2015). This implies that, for example, a system (e.g., dietary sample) comprised of four Noctua moths (Insecta, Lepidoptera) with even abundances has the same diversity as another system evenly composed of two Noctua moths and two Carabus beetles (Insecta, Coleoptera). Obviously, in ecological terms, a system holding moths and beetles is more heterogeneous than a system composed solely of moths. Although ecologists realized this pitfall long before molecular tools were implemented into the study of community ecology (Pielou, 1975), it was only in the last decade that a general framework of non‐ neutral measures of diversities was developed, based on the trait‐based or phylogenetic relations between detected OTUs (Chao, Chiu, et al., 2014a).
